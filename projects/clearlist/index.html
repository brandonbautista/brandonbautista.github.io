Clear the List: Spending the Last $10K Where It Mattered Most

A campaign built on a simple belief — that the most powerful thing a brand can do is prove it actually cares.

Budget
$10,000 — end-of-fiscal-year remainder
Timing
Back-to-school season, Michigan educators
Outcome
Organic reach, community goodwill, and zero regrets

The Situation

At the end of Michigan Virtual's fiscal year, we had $10,000 left in the advertising budget and a choice to make. The safe play was obvious: run one more awareness campaign, spend the money on impressions, show the line items to leadership, and call it a quarter.

The timing, though, was hard to ignore. It was back-to-school season — the time of year when Michigan teachers are setting up their classrooms, often spending their own money on supplies their schools can't afford. Teachers across the country post classroom wish lists online, hoping strangers will help fill the gaps.

I had a different idea for that $10,000.

The Idea

We invited Michigan educators to submit their classroom wish lists to us. Then we spread that $10,000 across as many of those lists as we could — directly funding the supplies teachers needed to start the school year.

It wasn't an awareness campaign. There were no banner ads, no click-through rates to optimize, no lead forms. It was Michigan Virtual putting money into Michigan classrooms and letting that action speak for itself.

The Pushback — and Why It Was Worth Navigating

Not everyone was immediately on board. The concern internally was reasonable on its face: would spending state-adjacent budget on teacher wish lists make us look like we had too much money? Would it invite scrutiny? Would it look tone-deaf rather than generous?

My read was the opposite. Michigan Virtual exists to serve Michigan's K-12 community. Teachers are our most important audience. Proving that we understood their daily reality — not just their enrollment decisions — was exactly the kind of signal that builds durable trust. An awareness campaign buys attention. This bought something harder to manufacture: credibility.

"The ones worth fighting for usually worked."

What Happened

The campaign generated significant organic reach and genuine community response — teachers sharing, colleagues tagging each other, goodwill that no paid campaign budget could have replicated. It became one of the most successful campaigns we ran that year, measured not in click-through rates but in the thing that actually matters: people feeling like Michigan Virtual was on their side.

It also validated a principle I've carried through every campaign since — that authenticity is a strategy, not just a value. When your brand does something real, the marketing takes care of itself.
